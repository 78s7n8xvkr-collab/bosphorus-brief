Life abroad runs on information. Whether you moved to İstanbul for work, İzmir for family, or Gaziantep for a project, the same questions follow you around: Is my residence paperwork about to change? What is the lira doing to my rent? What happened overnight, and does it matter for the people I care about? The Bosphorus Brief exists to answer those questions in one calm place, every day.

Who it's for

Internationals at home in Türkiye and the wider region — teachers, students, NGO and development professionals, entrepreneurs, researchers, remote workers, and families who have settled in for the long haul. If your life spans more than one country, this briefing is written for you.

What we cover

  • Türkiye — national news that shapes daily life.
  • Region — the wider neighbourhood, from the Levant to the Gulf.
  • Migration & Residency — permits, visas, citizenship rules, and policy changes that affect foreign residents.
  • Rights & Freedoms — courts and rulings, deportations and entry bans affecting foreign residents, and freedom of belief and press, for every community that calls the region home.
  • Safety — earthquakes, advisories, and security developments, reported steadily and without drama.
  • Economy — the lira, inflation, and what things cost.

How it works

The Brief is a reader, not a publisher. Every hour it gathers the latest reporting from established outlets and official feeds. When several outlets report the same event, it groups them into a single story and shows you the spread of coverage — who's reporting it, and who isn't. Each morning, Morning Çay — our AI-written digest — reads the day's material and brews it down to a few hundred words focused on one question: what does this mean for someone living here? Headlines link straight to the original reporting; the Brief never rewrites other people's journalism.

Our standards

  • Calm over clicks. No outrage, no doom. Safety news is specific and practical, never breathless.
  • Neutral by principle. We do not take sides in the politics of any country, and we do not attach religious, ethnic, or political labels to individuals or communities. People are neighbours, colleagues, and residents first.
  • Sources shown. Every story links to its original outlet, and the digest names what it read. Verify anything critical with the primary source — especially safety information.
  • Private by design. No accounts, no analytics, no cookies, no tracking of any kind. Your reading list and theme preference are stored only in your own browser and never leave it.

Sources

The feed currently draws on Hürriyet Daily News, Daily Sabah, Turkish Minute, BBC, Al Jazeera, Middle East Eye, The New Arab, InfoMigrants, ReliefWeb, UK FCDO travel advice, U.S. State Department advisories, Forum 18 and other rights and religious-liberty monitors, and topic searches across the wider press via Google News. Exchange rates are mid-market reference rates. Inclusion is not endorsement; read across outlets and make up your own mind.

Source lenses

News from this region reads differently depending on who owns the outlet. Where we can, each source carries a small label based on its ownership and affiliation — matters of public record, not our opinion of any article:

  • state — owned or funded by a government (TRT World, Anadolu Agency, Al Jazeera).
  • pro-gov — privately owned, with ownership close to the government of the day (Daily Sabah, Hürriyet Daily News).
  • opposition — opposition-leaning, or run by journalists in exile (Turkish Minute, Sözcü).
  • indep — independent newsrooms (Bianet, Medyascope, Al-Monitor).
  • intl — international outlets and wire services (BBC, Reuters, AP).
  • official — governments and international bodies publishing directly (travel advisories, ReliefWeb).

Grouped stories show a small coverage bar with these colours, and a story that several outlets are reporting from only one side of the spectrum is marked one-lens coverage — often the most useful signal on the page. Unlabelled sources are simply ones we haven't classified. None of this replaces your own judgement; it just tells you where to point it.

A note on the name

The Bosphorus divides two continents and joins two seas — which is a fair description of expat life, too. You stand in more than one world at once, and it helps to have a good view of both shores.
